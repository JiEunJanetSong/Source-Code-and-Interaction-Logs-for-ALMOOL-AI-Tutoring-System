Find all solutions of the IVP

$$
y^{\prime}=y+\frac{3}{y^2}, \quad y(0)=1
$$

}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

This is a nonlinear first-order differential equation, but it can be solved by using an appropriate substitution.

Step 1: Recognize the structure

We observe that this equation resembles the form:

$$
y^{\prime}=y+\frac{3}{y^2}
$$

which is not separable or linear, but a good strategy is to multiply both sides by $y^2$ to eliminate the denominator:

$$
y^2 y^{\prime}=y^3+3
$$


Now, let's try a substitution to simplify this equation.

Step 2: Substitution
Let:

$$
u=y^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad y=u^{1 / 3}
$$


But this makes things messy. Instead, look back to the equation:

$$
y^2 y^{\prime}=y^3+3
$$


Now try differentiating $y^3$ directly:

$$
\frac{d}{d t}\left(y^3\right)=3 y^2 y^{\prime} \Rightarrow y^2 y^{\prime}=\frac{1}{3} \frac{d}{d t}\left(y^3\right)
$$


So:

$$
\frac{1}{3} \frac{d}{d t}\left(y^3\right)=y^3+3 \Rightarrow \frac{d}{d t}\left(y^3\right)=3 y^3+9
$$

Step 3: Let $u=y^3 \Rightarrow u^{\prime}=3 y^2 y^{\prime}$

From above:

$$
u^{\prime}=3 y^2 y^{\prime}=3\left(y^3+3\right)=3 u+9
$$


Now we have a linear ODE:

$$
u^{\prime}=3 u+9
$$


Step 4: Solve the linear equation

Standard linear ODE:

$$
u^{\prime}-3 u=9
$$


Integrating factor:

$$
\mu(t)=e^{-3 t}
$$


Multiply both sides:

$$
e^{-3 t} u^{\prime}-3 e^{-3 t} u=9 e^{-3 t} \Rightarrow \frac{d}{d t}\left(e^{-3 t} u\right)=9 e^{-3 t}
$$


Integrate both sides:

$$
e^{-3 t} u=\int 9 e^{-3 t} d t=-3 e^{-3 t}+C \Rightarrow u(t)=-3+C e^{3 t}
$$


Step 5: Back-substitute to $y$
We had $u=y^3$, so:

$$
y(t)=\left(-3+C e^{3 t}\right)^{1 / 3}
$$


Step 6: Apply initial condition $y(0)=1$

$$
1=\left(-3+C \cdot e^0\right)^{1 / 3} \Rightarrow 1=(C-3)^{1 / 3} \Rightarrow C-3=1 \Rightarrow C=4
$$


Final Answer:

$$
y(t)=\left(-3+4 e^{3 t}\right)^{1 / 3}=\left(4 e^{3 t}-3\right)^{1 / 3}
$$


This is the unique solution to the IVP.

Find the limit.

$\lim _{\theta \rightarrow 0} \dfrac{\cos \theta-1}{\sin \theta}$

}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

We want
$
\lim_{\theta \to 0} \dfrac{\cos \theta - 1}{\sin \theta}.
$

A common way to see this limit is to use the small-angle approximations:
$
\cos \theta \approx 1 - \dfrac{\theta^2}{2}, 
\quad
\sin \theta \approx \theta,
$
for $\theta \to 0$. 

Then

$
\cos \theta - 1 
\approx \bigl(1 - \tfrac{\theta^2}{2}\bigr) - 1 
= -\dfrac{\theta^2}{2},
$

so
$
\dfrac{\cos \theta - 1}{\sin \theta} 
\approx \dfrac{-\tfrac{\theta^2}{2}}{\theta} 
= -\dfrac{\theta}{2}.
$

As $\theta \to 0$, this expression goes to $0$.

A more rigorous approach uses L'Hôpital’s Rule. 

Since
$\cos \theta - 1 \to 0$ and $\sin \theta \to 0$ as $\theta \to 0$, we can differentiate numerator and denominator:

$
\lim_{\theta \to 0} \dfrac{\cos \theta - 1}{\sin \theta}
\; \xrightarrow[\text{L'Hôpital}]{} \;
\lim_{\theta \to 0} \dfrac{\dfrac{d}{d\theta}(\cos \theta - 1)}{\dfrac{d}{d\theta}(\sin \theta)}
= \lim_{\theta \to 0} \dfrac{-\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}.
$

Evaluating at $\theta = 0$ gives
$
\dfrac{-\sin(0)}{\cos(0)} 
= \dfrac{0}{1} 
= 0.
$

Therefore,

$
\boxed{0}.
$

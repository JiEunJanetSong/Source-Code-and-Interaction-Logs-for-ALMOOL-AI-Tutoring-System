Find the derivative of the function.

$f(t)=\sin ^2\left(e^{\sin t}\right)$

}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

We want to differentiate 

$$
f(t) = \sin^2\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr).
$$

A good way to do this is to think of $f$ as a composition of two functions:

1. The **outer** function: $g(x) = \sin^2(x) = (\sin x)^2.$  

2. The **inner** function: $h(t) = e^{\sin t}.$

\textbf{Step 1: Differentiate the outer function $g(x) = (\sin x)^2$}

If $x$ is some expression, then

$$
\dfrac{d}{dx}\bigl[\sin^2(x)\bigr] 
= 2 \sin(x)\,\cos(x).
$$

\textbf{Step 2: Differentiate the inner function $h(t) = e^{\sin t}$}

We set $z = \sin t$. 

Then

$$
\dfrac{d}{dt}\bigl(e^z\bigr) = e^z \cdot \dfrac{dz}{dt} 
= e^z \cos t,
$$

and since $z = \sin t$, we substitute back $z = \sin t$:

$$
\dfrac{d}{dt}\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr) 
= e^{\sin t} \cos t.
$$

\textbf{Step 3: Apply the chain rule}

Putting it all together,

$$
\dfrac{df}{dt}
= \dfrac{d}{dx}\bigl[\sin^2(x)\bigr]\Big|_{x = e^{\sin t}}
 \;\times\; \dfrac{d}{dt}\bigl[e^{\sin t}\bigr].
$$

Hence,

$$
\dfrac{df}{dt}
= \underbrace{2 \sin\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr)\,\cos\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr)}_{\text{derivative of }\sin^2(x)\text{ at }x=e^{\sin t}}
 \;\times\; \underbrace{\left[e^{\sin t}\,\cos t\right]}_{\text{derivative of }e^{\sin t}}.
$$

So the derivative is:

$$
\boxed{
f'(t)
= 2 \,\sin\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr)\,\cos\bigl(e^{\sin t}\bigr)
\; e^{\sin t}\;\cos t.
}
$$

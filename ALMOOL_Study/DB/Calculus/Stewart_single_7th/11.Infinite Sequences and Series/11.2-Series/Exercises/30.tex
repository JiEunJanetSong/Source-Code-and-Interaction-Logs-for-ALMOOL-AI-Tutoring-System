Determine whether the series is convergent or divergent. If it is convergent, find its sum.

$$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{k(k+2)}{(k+3)^2}$$


}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

Step 1: Analyze the general term

Let's simplify the expression and analyze the behavior of the general term as $k \rightarrow \infty$:

$$
a_k=\dfrac{k(k+2)}{(k+3)^2}
$$


Multiply out the numerator and denominator:

- Numerator: $k(k+2)=k^2+2 k$

- Denominator: $(k+3)^2=k^2+6 k+9$

So:

$$
a_k=\dfrac{k^2+2 k}{k^2+6 k+9}
$$


Now compute the limit of the general term as $k \rightarrow \infty$ :

$$
\lim _{k \rightarrow \infty} \dfrac{k^2+2 k}{k^2+6 k+9}
$$


Divide numerator and denominator by $k^2$ :

$$
\dfrac{1+\dfrac{2}{k}}{1+\dfrac{6}{k}+\dfrac{9}{k^2}} \rightarrow \dfrac{1}{1}=1
$$

Step 2: Use the Divergence Test

The divergence test (a.k.a. nth-term test) states:

If $\lim _{k \rightarrow \infty} a_k \neq 0$, then the series diverges.

Here, since:

$$
\lim _{k \rightarrow \infty} \dfrac{k(k+2)}{(k+3)^2}=1 \neq 0
$$

the series must diverge.

Final Answer:

- The series is divergent, because the general term does not approach 0.

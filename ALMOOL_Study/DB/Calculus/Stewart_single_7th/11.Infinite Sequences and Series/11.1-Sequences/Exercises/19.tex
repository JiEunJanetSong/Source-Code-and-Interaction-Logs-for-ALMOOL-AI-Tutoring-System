Calculate, to four decimal places, the first ten terms of the sequence and use them to plot the graph of the sequence by hand. Does the sequence appear to have a limit? If so, calculate it. If not, explain why.

$$a_n=\frac{3n}{1+6n}$$

}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

We are given the sequence:

$
a_n = \frac{3n}{1 + 6n}
$

 Step 1: Calculate the First 10 Terms (to 4 decimal places)

Let’s compute $a_n$ for $n = 1$ to $10$:

$
\begin{align}
a_1 &= \frac{3(1)}{1 + 6(1)} = \frac{3}{7} \approx 0.4286 \\
a_2 &= \frac{6}{13} \approx 0.4615 \\
a_3 &= \frac{9}{19} \approx 0.4737 \\
a_4 &= \frac{12}{25} = 0.4800 \\
a_5 &= \frac{15}{31} \approx 0.4839 \\
a_6 &= \frac{18}{37} \approx 0.4865 \\
a_7 &= \frac{21}{43} \approx 0.4884 \\
a_8 &= \frac{24}{49} \approx 0.4898 \\
a_9 &= \frac{27}{55} \approx 0.4909 \\
a_{10} &= \frac{30}{61} \approx 0.4918 \\
\end{align}
$

Step 2: Graph by Hand (Description)

To plot this sequence by hand:

- Plot points $(n, a_n)$ for $n = 1$ through $10$.

- You'll see the points gradually increasing and getting closer to a certain horizontal value.

 Step 3: Does the Sequence Have a Limit?

Let’s find the limit:

$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n}{1 + 6n}
$

Divide numerator and denominator by $n$:

$
\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3}{\frac{1}{n} + 6} = \frac{3}{0 + 6} = \frac{1}{2}
$



 Final Answer:

- The sequence does have a limit.

- The limit is:

$
\boxed{\frac{1}{2}}
$

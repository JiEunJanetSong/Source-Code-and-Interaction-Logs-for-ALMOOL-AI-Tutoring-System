Calculate, to four decimal places, the first ten terms of the sequence and use them to plot the graph of the sequence by hand. Does the sequence appear to have a limit? If so, calculate it. If not, explain why.

$$a_n=1+\frac{10^n}{9^n}$$

}\SetValue{Concept}{%



}\SetValue{AltText}{%



}\SetValue{Solution}{%

We are given the sequence:

$
a_n = 1 + \frac{10^n}{9^n}
$

 Step 1: Simplify the Expression

We can write the fraction as a single exponential:

$
\frac{10^n}{9^n} = \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^n
$

So:

$
a_n = 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^n
$

Step 2: Calculate the First 10 Terms (to 4 decimal places)

We compute $a_n = 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^n$ for $n = 1$ to $10$:

$
\begin{align}
a_1 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^1 = 1 + 1.1111 = 2.1111 \\
a_2 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^2 = 1 + 1.2346 = 2.2346 \\
a_3 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^3 = 1 + 1.3717 = 2.3717 \\
a_4 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^4 = 1 + 1.5242 = 2.5242 \\
a_5 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^5 = 1 + 1.6936 = 2.6936 \\
a_6 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^6 = 1 + 1.8817 = 2.8817 \\
a_7 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^7 = 1 + 2.0908 = 3.0908 \\
a_8 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^8 = 1 + 2.3231 = 3.3231 \\
a_9 &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^9 = 1 + 2.5812 = 3.5812 \\
a_{10} &= 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^{10} = 1 + 2.8670 = 3.8670 \\
\end{align}
$

(Values rounded to 4 decimal places.)

Step 3: Plot the Sequence by Hand

- Plot points $(n, a_n)$ for $n = 1$ to $10$

- Youâ€™ll see the sequence increasing steadily

- The rate of increase gets faster over time

 Step 4: Does the Sequence Have a Limit?

We observe:

$
a_n = 1 + \left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^n
$

Since $\frac{10}{9} > 1$, then $\left( \frac{10}{9} \right)^n \to \infty$ as $n \to \infty$

So:

$
\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 1 + \infty = \infty
$

 Final Answer:

- First 10 terms (to 4 decimals): 

  $
  \boxed{2.1111,\ 2.2346,\ 2.3717,\ 2.5242,\ 2.6936,\ 2.8817,\ 3.0908,\ 3.3231,\ 3.5812,\ 3.8670}
  $

- Does the sequence have a limit?  

 No, the sequence increases without bound.

- Conclusion:  

  $
  \boxed{\text{The sequence diverges to infinity.}}
  $

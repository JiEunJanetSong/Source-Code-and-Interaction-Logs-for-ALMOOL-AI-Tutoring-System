Evaluate the integral.

$$\int \sqrt{1-4 x^2}\  d x$$

}\SetValue{Solution}{%

To evaluate

$$
\int \sqrt{1-4 x^2} d x
$$

we use a trigonometric substitution. Notice the integrand resembles $\sqrt{1-k^2 x^2}$ with $k=2$. Use

$$
\begin{aligned}
2 x=\sin \theta & \Longrightarrow x=\dfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta \\
d x & =\dfrac{1}{2} \cos \theta d \theta
\end{aligned}
$$


Then, the square root transforms as follows:

$$
\sqrt{1-4 x^2}=\sqrt{1-4\left(\dfrac{1}{2} \sin \theta\right)^2}=\sqrt{1-\sin ^2 \theta}=\cos \theta
$$


Substitute into the integral:

$$
\int \sqrt{1-4 x^2} d x=\int \cos \theta\left(\dfrac{1}{2} \cos \theta d \theta\right)=\dfrac{1}{2} \int \cos ^2 \theta d \theta
$$


Now, use the power-reduction identity:

$$
\cos ^2 \theta=\dfrac{1+\cos (2 \theta)}{2}
$$


Thus, the integral becomes

$$
\dfrac{1}{2} \int \dfrac{1+\cos (2 \theta)}{2} d \theta=\dfrac{1}{4} \int(1+\cos (2 \theta)) d \theta
$$

Integrate term by term:

$$
\int 1 d \theta=\theta, \quad \int \cos (2 \theta) d \theta=\dfrac{1}{2} \sin (2 \theta)
$$


Thus,

$$
\dfrac{1}{4}\left(\theta+\dfrac{1}{2} \sin (2 \theta)\right)+C
$$


Substitute back in terms of $x$. Since

$$
\sin \theta=2 x \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \theta=\arcsin (2 x),
$$

and using the double-angle formula,

$$
\sin (2 \theta)=2 \sin \theta \cos \theta
$$

with

$$
\cos \theta=\sqrt{1-\sin ^2 \theta}=\sqrt{1-4 x^2}
$$

we have

$$
\sin (2 \theta)=2(2 x) \sqrt{1-4 x^2}=4 x \sqrt{1-4 x^2}
$$


Thus, the antiderivative in terms of $x$ is

$$
\dfrac{1}{4} \arcsin (2 x)+\dfrac{1}{4} \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} \cdot 4 x \sqrt{1-4 x^2}+C=\dfrac{1}{4} \arcsin (2 x)+\dfrac{x}{2} \sqrt{1-4 x^2}+C
$$

The final result is

$$
\dfrac{1}{4} \arcsin (2 x)+\dfrac{x}{2} \sqrt{1-4 x^2}+C
$$

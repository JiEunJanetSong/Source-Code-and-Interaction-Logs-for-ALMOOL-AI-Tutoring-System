Evaluate the integral.

$$\int_{\sqrt{2}}^2 \ \dfrac{1}{t^3 \sqrt{t^2-1}}\  d t$$

}\SetValue{Solution}{%

We want to evaluate

$$
\int_{\sqrt{2}}^2 \dfrac{1}{t^3 \sqrt{t^2-1}} d t
$$


Observe that for $t>1$, a common trigonometric substitution for $\sqrt{t^2-1}$ is

$$
t=\sec (\theta), \quad \text { so that } \quad \sqrt{t^2-1}=\tan (\theta), \quad d t=\sec (\theta) \tan (\theta) d \theta
$$


Since $t=\sec (\theta)$, we also have $\dfrac{1}{t^3}=\cos ^3(\theta)$.
1) Rewrite the integrand via the substitution $t=\sec (\theta)$

Under $t=\sec (\theta)$,

$$
\sqrt{t^2-1}=\tan (\theta), \quad d t=\sec (\theta) \tan (\theta) d \theta, \quad \dfrac{1}{t^3}=\cos ^3(\theta)
$$


Hence the integrand becomes:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \dfrac{1}{t^3 \sqrt{t^2-1}} d t=\dfrac{\cos ^3(\theta)}{\tan (\theta)}[\sec (\theta) \tan (\theta) d \theta]=\cos ^3(\theta) \dfrac{\sec (\theta) \tan (\theta)}{\tan (\theta)}=\cos ^3(\theta) \sec (\theta)=\cos ^2(\theta) \\
& \text { Indeed, } \cos ^3(\theta) \sec (\theta)=\cos ^3(\theta) \dfrac{1}{\cos (\theta)}=\cos ^2(\theta)
\end{aligned}
$$

2) Change the integration limits

When $t=\sqrt{2}$ :

$$
\sec (\theta)=\sqrt{2} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \cos (\theta)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \theta=\dfrac{\pi}{4} .
$$


When $t=2$ :

$$
\sec (\theta)=2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \cos (\theta)=\dfrac{1}{2} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \theta=\dfrac{\pi}{3} \text {. }
$$


Thus the integral becomes

$$
\int_{\sqrt{2}}^2 \dfrac{1}{t^3 \sqrt{t^2-1}} d t=\int_{\theta=\pi / 4}^{\theta=\pi / 3} \cos ^2(\theta) d \theta
$$

3) Integrate $\cos ^2(\theta)$

A standard identity is

$$
\cos ^2(\theta)=\dfrac{1+\cos (2 \theta)}{2}
$$


Hence,

$$
\int \cos ^2(\theta) d \theta=\int \dfrac{1+\cos (2 \theta)}{2} d \theta=\dfrac{1}{2} \int 1 d \theta+\dfrac{1}{2} \int \cos (2 \theta) d \theta=\dfrac{\theta}{2}+\dfrac{1}{4} \sin (2 \theta)+C .
$$


Therefore,

$$
\int_{\pi / 4}^{\pi / 3} \cos ^2(\theta) d \theta=\left[\dfrac{\theta}{2}+\dfrac{1}{4} \sin (2 \theta)\right]_{\theta=\pi / 4}^{\theta=\pi / 3}
$$

3.1) Evaluate at $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$

$$
\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\dfrac{\pi / 3}{2}=\dfrac{\pi}{6}, \quad \sin \left(2 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)=\sin \left(\dfrac{2 \pi}{3}\right)=\sqrt{3} / 2
$$


Hence

$$
\dfrac{1}{4} \sin \left(2 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)=\dfrac{1}{4} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}
$$

So at $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$, the expression is

$$
\dfrac{\pi}{6}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}
$$

3.2) Evaluate at $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$

$$
\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\dfrac{\pi / 4}{2}=\dfrac{\pi}{8}, \quad \sin \left(2 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=\sin \left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)=1
$$


Hence

$$
\dfrac{1}{4} \sin \left(2 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)=\dfrac{1}{4}
$$


So at $\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$, the expression is

$$
\dfrac{\pi}{8}+\dfrac{1}{4}
$$

3.3) Subtract

$$
\left[\dfrac{\theta}{2}+\dfrac{1}{4} \sin (2 \theta)\right]_{\pi / 4}^{\pi / 3}=\left(\dfrac{\pi}{6}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}\right)-\left(\dfrac{\pi}{8}+\dfrac{1}{4}\right)=\left(\dfrac{\pi}{6}-\dfrac{\pi}{8}\right)+\left(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}-\dfrac{1}{4}\right) .
$$

- Combine the $\pi$-terms:

$$
\dfrac{\pi}{6}-\dfrac{\pi}{8}=\dfrac{4 \pi}{24}-\dfrac{3 \pi}{24}=\dfrac{\pi}{24}
$$

- Combine the constants:

$$
\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}-\dfrac{1}{4}=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}-\dfrac{2}{8}=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}-2}{8}
$$


Thus the definite integral is

$$
\dfrac{\pi}{24}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}-2}{8}
$$

(You may also write it as $\dfrac{\pi}{24}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{8}-\dfrac{1}{4}$; both forms are correct.)

Final Answer

$$
\int_{\sqrt{2}}^2 \dfrac{1}{t^3 \sqrt{t^2-1}} d t=\dfrac{\pi}{24}+\dfrac{\sqrt{3}-2}{8}
$$

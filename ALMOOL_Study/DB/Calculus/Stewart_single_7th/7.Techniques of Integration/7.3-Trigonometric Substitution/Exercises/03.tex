Evaluate the integral.

$$\int \dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x}\  d x $$

}\SetValue{Solution}{%

Step 1: Use a trigonometric substitution

Since we have a square root of the form $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$, let's use the substitution:

$$
x=2 \sec \theta \quad \Rightarrow \quad d x=2 \sec \theta \tan \theta d \theta
$$


Then:

- $\sqrt{x^2-4}=\sqrt{4 \sec ^2 \theta-4}=\sqrt{4\left(\sec ^2 \theta-1\right)}=\sqrt{4 \tan ^2 \theta}=2 \tan \theta$
- $\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x}=\dfrac{2 \tan \theta}{2 \sec \theta}=\tan \theta / \sec \theta=\sin \theta$

So the integrand becomes:

$$
\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x} d x=\sin \theta \cdot d x=\sin \theta \cdot(2 \sec \theta \tan \theta d \theta)
$$


Now use:

$$
\sin \theta=\dfrac{\tan \theta}{\sec \theta} \Rightarrow \text { So the product is } \dfrac{\tan \theta}{\sec \theta} \cdot 2 \sec \theta \tan \theta=2 \tan ^2 \theta
$$


Step 2: Integrate

$$
\int \dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{x} d x=\int 2 \tan ^2 \theta d \theta
$$


Recall the identity:

$$
\tan ^2 \theta=\sec ^2 \theta-1 \Rightarrow \int 2 \tan ^2 \theta d \theta=2 \int\left(\sec ^2 \theta-1\right) d \theta=2(\tan \theta-\theta)+C
$$


Step 3: Back-substitute

We had:

- $x=2 \sec \theta \Rightarrow \sec \theta=\dfrac{x}{2}$

- $\mathrm{So} \theta=\sec ^{-1}\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)$

- $\tan \theta=\sqrt{\sec ^2 \theta-1}=\sqrt{\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)^2-1}=\sqrt{\dfrac{x^2}{4}-1}=\dfrac{\sqrt{x^2-4}}{2}$

Now plug back in:

$$
2 \tan \theta-2 \theta=\sqrt{x^2-4}-2 \sec ^{-1}\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)
$$

Final Answer:

$$
\sqrt{x^2-4}-2 \sec ^{-1}\left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)+C
$$
